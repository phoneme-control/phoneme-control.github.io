Pronouncing Hard Words: Phoneme adaptation of a Pretrained Large Language Model-Based Speech Synthesis Model

Abstract

Modern end-to-end text-to-speech (TTS) systems produce natural and expressive speech, but explicit pronunciation control remains difficult for out-of-vocabulary words, named entities, and other pronunciation-sensitive spans. We study a general adaptation recipe for adding phoneme controllability to a pretrained LLM-based TTS model without retraining from scratch. The recipe extends the text vocabulary with ARPAbet special tokens, performs continued supervised fine-tuning on phoneme-augmented data, and applies pronunciation-focused direct preference optimization (DPO) with a frame mask centered on the forced-aligned target entity. The adapted model supports both phoneme-only prompts and hybrid prompts in which local phoneme spans are embedded inside ordinary text. On held-out drug names excluded from DPO preference construction, supervised phoneme adaptation achieves a 20.96% entity-span phoneme error rate, and DPO with SFT replay improves this to 11.53%. The final adapted model also outperforms the evaluated open- and closed-source TTS baselines on the drug-pronunciation benchmark. Beyond pronunciation accuracy, Seed-TTS-Eval performance is preserved, but DPO can shift speaking rate and weaken expressive control, motivating evaluation of phoneme adaptation as a multi-objective problem.
